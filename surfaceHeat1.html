Methodology

The data used in this story were collected by an instrument called MODIS, which stands for Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer. It’s one of five specialized instruments on board a NASA satellite called TERRA, whose mission is focused on watching the Earth.

MODIS takes pictures of the land surface twice a day—once in the morning and once at night and records the images as pixels, just like a cell phone camera. A pixel, in this case, is a square that represents an area of land 1 kilometer wide by 1 kilometer high, an area roughly 247 acres big or half the size of California’s Disneyland. The images have a value associated with it that represents the average daytime land surface temperature (LST) for each year from 2013 to 2023, during the hottest months of the year (May 1st through September 30th).

There are a lot of different surfaces in an area that large. Rooftops, streets, sidewalks, parking lots, and back yards, each of which has a different surface temperature. That difference is averaged by the satellite and combined into a single number.
